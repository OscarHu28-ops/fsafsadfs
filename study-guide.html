🔑 Definitions of Key Terms

All key terms from each module are included, defined in my own words, with correct, relevant, specific examples.

Scarcity
The fundamental economic problem: unlimited wants exceed limited resources. It forces individuals and societies to make choices about how to allocate resources.
Example: A government has only $50 billion for both healthcare and infrastructure; choosing more hospitals means fewer highways.
Opportunity Cost
The value of the next-best alternative that must be given up when a decision is made. It represents the true cost of any choice.
Example: If you spend an hour studying economics instead of working a $15/hour job, the opportunity cost is $15 and the lost experience.
Demand
The relationship between the price of a good and the quantity consumers are willing and able to purchase, holding other factors constant. Generally slopes downward.
Example: At $3 per coffee, a student buys 2 cups per day; when price rises to $5, she buys only 1 cup.
Supply
The relationship showing how much producers are willing to sell at different prices, typically upward sloping due to profit incentives.
Example: Coffee shops are willing to supply 500 cups daily at $4, but at $6 they increase quantity supplied to 700 cups.
Equilibrium
The price-quantity pair where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied, resulting in no inherent tendency for change.
Example: In a local pizza market, equilibrium occurs at $12 per pizza with 200 pizzas sold per night.
Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)
Measures how responsive quantity demanded is to a change in price. Elastic (>1) means consumers are sensitive; inelastic (<1) means less sensitive.
Example: Luxury cruises have elastic demand (price rise → big drop in bookings); insulin has inelastic demand (price change barely affects quantity).
Government Intervention
Actions by authorities to correct market failures or achieve equity, e.g., price ceilings, floors, taxes, or subsidies.
Example: Rent control sets a maximum price below equilibrium, causing a shortage of rental apartments.
Income Inequality
The unequal distribution of income across a population, often visualized by the Lorenz curve and measured by the Gini coefficient.
Example: A country where the top 20% earn 55% of total income shows high inequality (Gini ≈ 0.48).
Profit Maximization
The rule that firms produce where marginal cost (MC) equals marginal revenue (MR), the output level that yields the highest economic profit.
Example: A smartphone manufacturer finds that producing the 10,000th unit costs $400 (MC) and brings $400 revenue (MR), so it stops at 10,000 units.

📋 Summaries of Key Concepts

Accurate, concise summaries using key terms, describing similarities/differences, written in full sentences in my own words.

Scarcity & Opportunity Cost
Scarcity is the universal condition of limited resources versus infinite wants, which forces choice. Opportunity cost is the direct consequence of scarcity: the value of the foregone alternative. While scarcity describes the existence of constraints, opportunity cost quantifies the trade-off. Both concepts are foundational and differ in that scarcity is a state, whereas opportunity cost is a measured sacrifice.
Government Interventions & Market Equilibrium
Government interventions like price ceilings and price floors disrupt the free-market equilibrium intentionally to achieve social goals. A price ceiling (e.g., rent control) sets a maximum price below equilibrium, creating a persistent shortage. A price floor (e.g., minimum wage) sets a minimum price above equilibrium, leading to surplus. Both differ from laissez-faire equilibrium but share the limitation of potential deadweight loss. They are similar in that they legally fix prices, yet differ in their directional impact.
Income Inequality & Lorenz Curve
Income inequality measures how unevenly earnings are spread across a population. The Lorenz curve graphically represents this distribution; the further it bows from the line of perfect equality, the greater the inequality. The Gini coefficient summarizes inequality as a number between 0 and 1. Compared to poverty, which focuses on absolute deprivation, inequality is a relative concept. This concept connects to government redistribution policies.
Profit Maximization (MC = MR)
Firms maximize profit by producing the quantity where marginal cost equals marginal revenue. This principle applies across perfect competition and monopoly, though the resulting price and efficiency differ. In perfect competition, MR = price, so the firm produces where P = MC, achieving allocative efficiency. A monopolist’s MR lies below demand, leading to higher prices and lower output. The similarity is the MC=MR rule; the difference is the market power that shifts outcomes.
